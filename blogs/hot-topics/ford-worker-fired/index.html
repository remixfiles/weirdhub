Ford Worker Fired Over $1.95 Cookie He Paid For: A Tale of Corporate Oversight and Redemption

By WeirdHub • 11 July, 2026

A Routine Night Shift Turns into Career-Ending Accusation

Kurt Kromm, a 60-year-old diabetic electrician with 11 years of dedicated service at Ford Motor Company’s Kentucky Truck Plant in Louisville, faced an abrupt end to his career on May 16, 2026, after being accused of stealing a $1.95 pack of Grandma’s Chocolate Chip Cookies. During his overnight shift on May 9 around 3:30 a.m., Kromm experienced a dangerous drop in blood sugar, prompting him to purchase the cookies from an Aramark self-checkout kiosk in the plant’s break room to stabilize his glucose levels, which had fallen into the low 60s according to his continuous glucose monitor. He swiped his debit card at the first machine, which displayed a red failure screen, then tried a second kiosk where the transaction appeared to process. Believing the payment had gone through, he ate the cookies and returned to work repairing robots and automated equipment. A week later, supervisors summoned him to the labor office, showed surveillance footage highlighting the initial failed transaction, and terminated him immediately under the company’s zero-tolerance theft policy. Security escorted him out of the facility without allowing him to retrieve his personal tools or belongings, leaving Kromm stunned after years of perfect attendance, high performance, and earning over $200,000 annually while commuting from his home in Kenosha, Wisconsin.

Proving Innocence Amid Bureaucratic Hurdles and Company Reversal

Determined to clear his name, Kromm reviewed his bank statements and discovered the $1.95 charge had indeed posted at 3:38 a.m. He provided screenshots to Ford and his United Auto Workers (UAW) representative, but the company demanded notarized copies of the full statements for verification. After further checks with Aramark, the operator of the plant’s vending system, Ford confirmed the payment had processed correctly and acknowledged the termination as wrongful. Approximately seven weeks after the firing, the automaker offered Kromm reinstatement to his original 7 p.m. to 7 a.m. shift along with roughly $28,000 in back pay for the lost time—slightly less than the $33,000 initially indicated by union officials. Kromm also received confirmation from Kentucky’s Office of Unemployment Insurance that he qualified for benefits during the dispute. The incident highlighted potential glitches in the self-checkout kiosks, which workers reported frequently malfunction, sometimes leading to items being left unpaid without immediate repercussions. Ford later agreed to revise its policy, opting for temporary suspensions and investigations rather than immediate terminations in future vending disputes.

New Chapter for Kromm as Ford Loses a Valued Employee

Despite the offer to return, Kromm declined reinstatement, citing emotional distress, lack of a formal apology, and the damage to his trust after being branded a thief and later questioned on his documentation. He had already secured a higher-paying electrician position near his Kenosha home in Wisconsin, starting the day after Memorial Day with improved compensation and no long commute. Kromm plans only a brief return to Louisville to collect his tools and personal items. Colleagues described him as an excellent, helpful worker who went above and beyond, including advocating for others and serving on the plant’s Emergency Rescue Team. The case has sparked widespread discussion about corporate policies, union support, and the human cost of automated systems in high-stakes manufacturing environments. Ford declined to comment on the individual case but stated it seeks to rectify situations handled differently and values its employees. Aramark affirmed cooperation with investigations while focusing on convenient services. Kromm, who holds degrees in political science, economics, and mediation, expressed sadness over the unexpected end to what he viewed as a long-term career at Ford, a company he once loved. The episode serves as a cautionary tale about balancing strict policies with fairness in the workplace. (Word count: 698)
